Leading-edge IFNγ and TNF Module Scores in Enterocyte Populations Pre- and Post-Ruxolitinib

1 Overview

This report addresses the question:

Can we use the leading-edge genes from significant IFNγ- and TNF-related GSEA pathways in enterocyte populations to generate IFNγ and TNF module scores before and after ruxolitinib treatment as an additional output of the dataset?

This report is designed to provide a donor-level summary that complements the GSEA results.

Note

Two key points are important for interpretation:

  1. GSEA and module scores are not the same statistic. GSEA detects coordinated rank shifts across pathway genes, while module scores summarize the average behavior of a selected gene set in cells.

  2. IFNγ and TNF may behave differently. IFNγ-related genes often move coherently and are therefore well captured by module scores. TNF-related pathways can contain mixed regulatory and feedback genes, so significant GSEA results may not always translate into equally strong module-score separation.

13 Interpretation

13.1 IFNγ

If IFNγ leading-edge module scores decrease after treatment in enterocyte populations, that provides a useful donor-level summary consistent with IFNγ GSEA findings.

Because IFNγ pathways are often transcriptionally coherent, these module scores are usually easier to interpret directly.

13.2 TNF

TNF leading-edge module scores should be interpreted more cautiously.

TNF-related GSEA signals can remain biologically meaningful even if donor-level module scores are weaker or not formally significant. This can happen because TNF pathways often include receptor signaling, feedback regulators, and mixed-direction genes, so average module scores may partially cancel coordinated rank-based enrichment that is still detected by GSEA.

Therefore, TNF module scores are best treated as an additional descriptive output, not as a replacement for the original GSEA result.
